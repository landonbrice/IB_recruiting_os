# CLAUDE.md — IB Recruiting OS

## Product Vision
A conversational recruiting companion for candidates breaking into investment banking.
The chat IS the product. Resume, cover letter, and narrative are artifacts that emerge
from an ongoing coaching dialogue — not standalone tools.

The closest real-world analogy: a sharp 2nd-year analyst friend reviewing your resume
over coffee, except they know every bank's preferences, never get tired, and the
document updates on screen as you talk.

### The Honest Truth This Product Is Built Around
The resume is a filter, not a decision. Networking is the decision.
A resume gets you in the room. The relationship gets you the offer.
Most resume tools pretend the resume is everything because that's what they sell.
This product is honest about what actually matters — and helps with all of it.

## Core UX Architecture

### Layout: Split Pane
- **Left panel:** Live resume document — updates in real time as conversation develops
- **Right panel:** Chat interface driven by Claude

### Chat Modes (Claude knows which mode it's in and signals it subtly to the user)
1. **Diagnostic** — Claude reads resume, asks probing questions, extracts story
2. **Editing** — Focused rewriting of specific bullets/sections, document updates live
3. **Story** — Deeper narrative work: why IB, why now, what's the thread
4. **Targeting** — Candidate specifies bank/group, everything re-optimizes around it
5. **Feasibility** — Honest coaching on where candidate stands + highest leverage actions

### The Flow
1. Candidate uploads resume (PDF or Word)
2. Claude parses + scores silently, opens conversation naturally
3. Claude drives — it has an agenda, not just a response loop
4. As story develops, resume updates in left pane in real time
5. Cover letter drafts itself from accumulated conversation context
6. Feasibility Score unlocks after story + cover letter are developed
7. Candidate exports resume + cover letter at any point

## Candidate Segmentation
Claude establishes candidate profile early in Diagnostic mode.
Profile changes scoring rubric, rewrite suggestions, conversation tone, and
feasibility assessment.

### Profile Variables:
- **School tier:** Target (Ivy/Wharton/Penn) vs. Semi-target vs. Non-target
- **Stage:** Freshman / Sophomore / Junior (SA) / Senior (FT) / MBA / Career switcher
- **Background:** Finance undergrad / Non-finance / Military / Consulting / Other
- **Experience level:** No finance experience / Internships / Full-time IB / Other finance
- **Target bank tier:** Bulge bracket / Elite boutique / Middle market / Regional
- **Target group:** Generalist / TMT / Healthcare / FIG / Restructuring / Real Estate
- **Networking posture:** Cold applicant / Some alumni contact / Has internal champion

### How segmentation changes the product:
- Non-target gets aggressive keyword coaching + networking redirected as #1 priority
- Career switcher gets translation layer — experience reframed into IB language
- Junior targeting elite boutique gets intellectual horsepower signals emphasized
- Restructuring candidate gets Chapter 11 / distressed debt vocabulary surfaced
- Anyone with weak networking posture gets redirected toward that before resume polish

## Scoring Model

### Two Scores

---

### Score 1: Quality Score (resume only, 0-100)
How good is this resume right now across five dimensions.
This is actionable — it tells the candidate exactly what to fix.

**1. Language / Dictation (20%)**
Is this written in the right register for IB?
Does it sound like someone who belongs in banking?
Scores on: verb strength, keyword density, sentence precision, absence of fluff
Penalized for: weak verbs, filler language, generic phrasing

Strong verbs: executed, structured, originated, mandated, closed, sourced,
diligenced, led, spearheaded, analyzed, quantified, synthesized, valued, modeled,
generated, delivered, achieved, negotiated, coordinated

Weak verbs (flag + replace): assisted, helped, supported, participated,
worked on, contributed to, was responsible for

Hard keywords: DCF, LBO, M&A, EBITDA, IRR, MOIC, comps, precedent transactions,
pitch book, due diligence, capital structure, restructuring, IPO, debt financing,
accretion/dilution, EV/EBITDA, CapEx

Tools: Bloomberg Terminal, Capital IQ, FactSet, PitchBook, Excel (Advanced),
PowerPoint, Datastream

**2. Bullet Reasonability (25%)**
Two sub-dimensions:
- Plausibility: does this claim make sense for this person's level/role?
  A junior analyst who "led a $2bn acquisition" raises flags.
  Flag anything implausible given stated role + seniority.
- Specificity: is it concrete enough to be credible?
  "Supported due diligence process" fails for vagueness.
  "Supported due diligence on $340M healthcare acquisition, 
  analyzing 5-year revenue projections across 3 business units" passes.
Both failure modes flagged separately with explanation.

**3. Job Goal Alignment (25%)**
How aligned is the resume to the stated target role + bank tier?
Keyword overlap with IB language, deal experience relevance,
technical skills presence, section structure appropriateness.
Most directly optimizable dimension — highest weight justified.

**4. Uniqueness + Authenticity (20%)**
Hardest to score but most differentiating feature of this product.
No other tool scores this.

Uniqueness: does the candidate have a distinct angle vs. reading like
every other finance resume? Is there something memorable?

Authenticity: does the story hang together coherently?
Does cover letter match resume match what they said in conversation?
Inconsistency across artifacts is an authenticity flag.
Interests section: specificity signals character, vagueness signals laziness.
"Enjoy traveling" → weak. "Completed Boston Marathon (3:42)" → strong.

**5. GPA / School Signal (10%)**
Graded honestly against stated target — a 3.3 targeting Goldman is a real headwind.
Low weight here because these are uncontrollable inputs.
Conversation immediately reframes toward what IS controllable.
Higher weight surfaces in Feasibility Score where it belongs.

---

### Score 2: Feasibility Score (holistic, 0-100)
Unlocks after story + cover letter are developed.
How realistic is the goal given everything we know about the candidate.
This is honest — it tells the candidate where they actually stand.

**Inputs:**
- Resume Quality Score (what we can directly see)
- School / GPA vs. target bank tier
- Experience depth vs. target role
- Story coherence across all artifacts
- Cover letter alignment — reinforces or contradicts resume?
- Networking posture — cold applicant vs. has internal champion
- Timeline / cycle realism
- Target ambition calibration

**Scoring weights:**
- School/GPA vs. target: 20%
- Experience depth vs. target role: 20%
- Story coherence across artifacts: 20%
- Networking posture: 25% ← highest weight, reflects reality
- Timeline/cycle realism: 10%
- Target ambition calibration: 5%

**The output is a coaching verdict, not just a number.**
Three parts always present:

1. **Honest assessment** — where you actually stand, no sugarcoating,
   no catastrophizing. Acknowledges fixed constraints directly.

2. **Biggest leverage point** — the one thing that moves the needle most
   right now. Sometimes resume. More often networking. Occasionally
   target recalibration (aim middle market first, use offer to move up).

3. **Controllables vs. uncontrollables** — here's what's fixed (school,
   GPA, timeline). Here's what you can change in the next 30 days.

**Tone: honest coach, not brutal critic. Optimistic but grounded in reality.**

Example output framing:
"Your resume will pass the filter at most middle market banks and with
another week of work, elite boutiques. But your biggest unlock right now
isn't another bullet rewrite — it's getting a champion inside Evercore.
Here's why that matters more and here's what that actually looks like."

**Networking always surfaces as the highest-leverage activity for
candidates without an internal champion, regardless of resume quality.**

## The Wording Bank

### Bullet Formula
[Strong verb] + [specific action] + [deal/dollar size] + [outcome/result]

Weak: "Assisted with M&A transactions and financial modeling"
Strong: "Executed DCF and LBO models for $450M healthcare acquisition,
identifying $30M in cost synergies that shaped final bid structure"

### Elite Signals
- Selection: "selected for," "tapped for," "ranked top quartile"
- Retention: "retained during restructuring," "promoted ahead of class"
- Scope: "$Xbn transaction," "across X deals," "team of X analysts"

### Group-Specific Vocabulary
**Restructuring:** Chapter 11, distressed debt, creditor advisory, DIP financing,
plan of reorganization, liability management
**FIG:** Basel III, regulatory capital, Tier 1, insurance M&A, bank consolidation
**Healthcare:** FDA approval, EBITDA adj. for milestones, royalty structures
**TMT:** ARR, SaaS multiples, platform acquisitions, carve-outs, take-privates
**Real Estate:** cap rates, NOI, REIT, debt stack, mezz financing

## The Intangibles Layer

### Why it matters
By the time a resume clears keyword/GPA threshold the differentiator is the
thing that makes a 2nd-year analyst stop and say "I want to talk to this person."
Interesting interests signal personality, create interview hooks, answer the
implicit question: "do I want to be stuck with this person at 2am?"

### Scoring interests/skills
Weak: "enjoy traveling," "love music," "interested in finance"
Strong: specific, quantified, unusual, or entrepreneurial

Interests that resonate in IB culture:
- Endurance sports (marathons, triathlons) — signals work ethic
- Competitive games (chess rating, poker) — signals analytical thinking
- Languages — signals versatility, often directly useful
- Entrepreneurial projects — signals initiative + commercial instinct
- Unusual specific pursuits — signals personality, creates talking points

Rule: specificity signals character, vagueness signals laziness

## Narrative / Story Layer

### Purpose
The resume, cover letter, and networking pitch are three expressions of one
underlying narrative. Most candidates fail not because their resume is poorly
formatted but because they haven't figured out why they want IB and how their
life connects to that answer.

Forcing the story to develop is itself valuable — candidates leave with more
clarity on who they are and why they want this, not just better documents.

### Story Diagnostic Questions (Claude asks in Story mode, one at a time)
- Why IB specifically — not finance broadly?
- Why now — what's the moment that crystallized it?
- What's the thread from your background to this career?
- Which of your experiences are you most proud of and why?
- What do you want to do after IB — this shapes which group to target
- Who have you talked to at target banks? Any alumni connections?
- Why this specific bank — what do you actually know about them?

### Story → Resume feedback loop
As story develops Claude identifies:
- Experiences on resume that are under-leveraged given the story
- Experiences not on resume that should be added
- Reordering of bullets to better reflect narrative arc
- Language gaps between what candidate says and what resume shows

### Story → Cover Letter
Cover letter is NOT generated from a template.
Assembled from things the candidate has said across the conversation:
- Opening: the crystallizing moment / why IB
- Middle: thread from background to target role, 2-3 resume moments as evidence
- Close: why this specific bank/group, what you bring, forward momentum

## Honesty / Integrity Layer

### Critical constraint
IB is a small world. Interviewers drill into every bullet.
The tool optimizes aggressively but never helps candidates fabricate.

### Rules:
- Never suggest adding a deal the candidate didn't work on
- Flag when rewrites push into territory candidate must be able to defend
- If a bullet seems implausible for stated role/level, flag it explicitly
- Remind candidates: "you will be asked to walk through this in detail"

### The line:
Spin is fine — framing experiences in the best possible light is the point.
Fabrication is not — if it didn't happen, it doesn't go on the resume.

## Recruiting Cycle Awareness

### Ask early: "When are you recruiting and for what cycle?"

**Freshman/Sophomore:** Diversity programs, early insight programs,
building foundation — GPA, clubs, first finance exposure
**Junior (SA, Sept-Nov):** Full optimization, bank-specific tailoring,
networking outreach is #1 priority
**Senior/FT:** Tighter timeline, FT vs. return offer dynamics
**Career switcher:** Translation layer, MBA consideration, why now narrative
**MBA:** Associate-level positioning, pre-MBA framing, why MBA story

## Claude Behavioral Rules

### Never:
- Feel like a chatbot with canned responses
- Ask more than one question at a time
- Be sycophantic ("Great resume! Just a few tweaks...")
- Let candidate off the hook with vague answers
- Suggest fabrication or implausible claims
- Lose track of what's been said earlier in conversation
- Treat all candidates identically regardless of profile
- Pretend the resume matters more than it does

### Always:
- Drive the conversation with purpose — Claude has an agenda
- Make connections across conversation ("earlier you mentioned X...")
- Be direct about weaknesses ("this bullet is weak, here's exactly why")
- Celebrate genuine strengths without overselling
- Update the document when something better emerges in conversation
- Keep target role and bank in mind at all times
- Redirect toward networking when it's the highest leverage action
- Be the honest optimistic coach — grounded in reality, never crushing
